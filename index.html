The Future of Visual Arts
The Past, Present, and Future of Technology in Art
Context

The purpose of visual arts is to express and evoke feelings through the use of perceptible forms, such as painting, film-making, sculpting, and many others. The practice has been around since the Stone Age when the first examples of art were mere cave paintings and carvings. Over time, art evolved, and people began to express themselves through different styles and methods. Visual art is meant to resonate with a person by using core concepts such as color, subject, and shape in order to invoke certain feelings. The goal of our website is to inform people about the world of technology in visual art by showing the past creations of this topic and making our own hypothetical technological innovation.



    Timeline
    This timeline consists of the past and current technological creations surrounding art, including hardware and software.

  • Stylus pens

    1957

    Stylus pens mimic the feeling of real pencils/pens while using art software. Most stylus pens work on all touch screens and occasionally have their own device it was customly made for.

  • 3D printers

    1980s

    3D printers can print out 3D models of art made by using sculpting software. There are many uses to these printers such as creating product models, prototypes, and customly made art pieces.

  • Drawing tablets

    1984

    Drawing tablets provide a flat surface for digital drawing, mirrors whatever is on screen; needs to be hooked up to a device before use.

  • PhotoShop

    1987

    PhotoShop lets users edit and manipulate videos, images, and other various elements on a 2D and 3D plane.

  • Poser Pro

    1995

    Poser Pro helps users learn animation by providing characters models, scenes, props, textures, lighting, and much more.

  • Krita

    2005

    Krita is a free drawing and animation program that provides users with an array of digital painting/sketching tools.

  • Procreate

    2011

    Procreate is a digital painting software that allows users to create customizable brushes, as well as make their own color palettes amongst other features found in various drawing apps.

Future Innovations
Possible Tech Inventions and Ideas

This section details the possible ideas that we had for the future of visual arts. In order to create fast and efficient appliances that everyone has access to, we have created multiple technological innovations that we think could help with that. Not only would they improve performance, but they would also make the process of creating art easier.


Our main idea is to have an art device called VAV, where users can create basic scenes and then project the image in hologram form. The purpose of this device is to help with learning anatomy and animation. The user would create the scene on the device with the provided models such as people, animals, trees, and other objects. When the scene is done the device will be able to project the scene as a 3D hologram out of a port located on the back. While using the provided MOCAP gloves the user will be able to physically edit and change the hologram to their liking. The hologram can also be edited by manually using the device. VAV is relatively the same size as an Ipad and is lightweight, ensuring easy travel.



Other smaller ideas:

  • A possible innovation would be an app that automates line art. The user would be able to upload their sketch to the app and it will instantly create line art for the sketch. There would also be a feature that allows the user to alter the drawing and change the type of brush used to create the line art.
  • Another would be a pen that can pick up colors from the environment by touching different surfaces. An example of this would be making the pen touch a green leaf, then the pen will illustrate in green.
  • MOCAP suits (motion capture/CGI suits) that have separable parts are also a possibility. The suit would be connected by zippers at the hands, feet, limbs, and other parts of the body so that if the user would want to reference the motion of the hand, they would just wear the hand.
  • A thin screen that resembles a piece of paper. Users would be able to draw on it with pencil or other graphite tools and then refresh the paper making it blank again. The paper would also be able to undo actions like a normal drawing program.
